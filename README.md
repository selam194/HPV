# HPV
HPV Screening Data Analysis and Visualization Project
Project Overview
Conducted an end-to-end analysis of an 8-year Human Papillomavirus (HPV) screening dataset collected from over 25 health facilities in Addis Ababa, Ethiopia. The dataset consisted of 1,100 cervical cancer screening specimens from female patients undergoing routine screening and follow-up testing. The objective was to uncover epidemiological trends, assess screening performance, and generate actionable insights to support cervical cancer prevention efforts.
Tools & Technologies
•	Python
•	Pandas
•	NumPy
•	Matplotlib
•	Seaborn
•	VS Code
•	Data Cleaning & Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)
Key Activities
•	Imported and configured analytical libraries for data processing and visualization.
•	Loaded and explored the dataset to understand structure, quality, and variable relationships.
•	Renamed and transformed columns into appropriate data types for efficient analysis.
•	Identified and cleaned missing values to improve data reliability.
•	Performed comprehensive Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA).
•	Developed visualizations to highlight screening trends, HPV prevalence, age distribution, turnaround times, and genotype patterns.
Key Findings
•	Identified an overall HPV positivity rate of 33%, reflecting a prevalence level consistent with reports from many Sub-Saharan African populations.
•	Found that new screenings and two-year follow-up screenings represented the largest screening groups, suggesting strong patient retention and ongoing participation in cervical cancer screening programs.
•	Determined that the highest screening volume occurred among women aged 30–49 years, aligning with WHO cervical cancer screening recommendations.
•	Analyzed laboratory performance and found specimen turnaround times clustered around 47 days.
•	Revealed that Other High-Risk HPV (HR-HPV) genotypes were the most prevalent category, accounting for the majority of positive cases.
•	Detected HPV-16 in approximately 45 cases (~13% of positive samples) and HPV-18 in approximately 38 cases (~11% of positive samples), both of which are strongly associated with cervical cancer development.
•	Identified a subset of patients with multiple HPV genotype co-infections, which may indicate increased risk of disease progression.
•	Demonstrated the importance of extended HPV genotyping beyond HPV-16 and HPV-18 for improved patient risk stratification and clinical decision-making.
Impact
This analysis transformed raw screening data into meaningful public health insights, providing a clearer understanding of HPV prevalence, screening utilization patterns, and genotype distribution. The findings can support evidence-based cervical cancer prevention strategies, screening program optimization, and future epidemiological research initiatives.
